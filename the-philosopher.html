The Philosopher

Jordan Peterson

Rene Descartes

  • Attention: René Descartes and Jordan Peterson both capture attention by challenging what people assume to be true. Descartes opened his Meditations with the radical claim that everything he had ever believed could be false, instantly creating philosophical tension that forced readers to engage. Similarly, Peterson opens lectures and interviews by challenging modern assumptions about identity, meaning, and society — statements like "sort yourself out before criticizing the world" provoke immediate reaction, whether agreement or outrage. Both figures understood that the fastest way to grab attention is to destabilize certainty.
  • Retention: Descartes and Peterson retain their audience by walking them through a logical journey step by step. Descartes built his philosophy like a structured argument — each Meditation depended on the last, so readers had to keep going to see where the reasoning landed. Peterson does the same in long-form conversations and lectures, layering ideas from psychology, mythology, and religion into a single thread that keeps the listener thinking "where is he going with this?" In both cases retention comes from the feeling that something important is being built, and leaving early means missing the conclusion.
  • Distribution: Descartes spread his ideas through handwritten letters and printed books circulated among European intellectuals, relying on academic networks and controversy to amplify his reach. Peterson uses YouTube, podcasts, and clips that get shared across social media, where the algorithm rewards debate and strong opinions. Both figures benefited from controversy as a distribution mechanism — people who disagreed spread the ideas just as much as people who agreed.
