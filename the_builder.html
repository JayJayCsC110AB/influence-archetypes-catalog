  • Attention: Leonardo Da Vinci and Mark Rober both capture attention by making the impossible look achievable. Da Vinci filled his notebooks with designs for flying machines, armored vehicles, and solar power centuries before the technology existed — the sheer ambition of his ideas made people stop and stare. Mark Rober does the same with videos like building the world's largest Nerf gun or engineering glitter bomb traps for porch pirates — the concept alone is so outrageous that you have to click to see if it actually works. Both lead with a premise so ambitious it feels like it shouldn't be possible.
  • Retention: Both figures retain their audience by walking them through the process step by step. Da Vinci's notebooks weren't just final designs — they were filled with sketches, failed attempts, and iterative thinking that showed how his mind worked through a problem. Rober structures his videos the same way, taking the viewer through the engineering process — what went wrong, what he adjusted, and how he finally solved it — so the payoff at the end feels earned. In both cases the journey is as interesting as the result.
  • Distribution: Da Vinci spread his ideas through patrons like Ludovico Sforza and Lorenzo de' Medici, wealthy figures who funded his work and displayed it publicly, giving his inventions and paintings visibility across Italian courts and beyond. Rober distributes through YouTube where his videos consistently hit tens of millions of views, amplified by media coverage and school teachers who share his content as educational material. Both benefited from institutional backing — Da Vinci through patronage, Rober through a platform that rewards quality and an audience that shares his videos far beyond his subscriber base.
